AI Credit-Card Advisor Passed

An assistant that tells you which card to use for each purchase, when to redeem, and when to enroll in offers. I wrote the proposal, ran the numbers, and passed.

  • 90%of revenue per user comes from affiliate — $20 of $22
  • $17ka year for the trust-pure model, with no affiliate and no bank credentials
  • 1testable dependency the whole proposition reduces to

What it is

The target user is a US points maximizer carrying three or more rewards cards. The job is not information — that already exists everywhere. It is effortless, trustworthy optimization at the moment of payment.

This one never got built. It got a proposal, a business case, and a recommendation.

The decision

I made the tension arithmetic instead of adjectival.

The pitch writes itself: an advisor you can trust because it doesn't take affiliate money. But affiliate money is where the revenue is. Strip it out and the only real differentiator earns $17k a year. That is a hobby, not a business.

And the six-figure model reintroduces exactly the conflict of interest the pitch is built on rejecting. There is no version of the numbers where strong trust positioning and a real revenue line coexist comfortably.

Once that was written as arithmetic rather than as a concern, the decision stopped being a matter of taste. Everything else — on-device context without bank credentials — reduces to a single testable dependency, and if it fails, the product is a worse CardPointers.

What it cost

The recommendation was pass, with at most a two-week time-boxed explore of a small-business niche. Passing means giving up a real market and a genuinely interesting problem, and it means the work produced a memo instead of an app.

I've kept it here deliberately. The projects that didn't get built are the ones where the reasoning is easiest to check, because there's no shipped product distracting from it.

Status & next

Closed. Revisit only if the on-device context dependency becomes cheap, which would change the arithmetic rather than the argument.
