Split Decision Beta

A daily phrase puzzle with two ways to play. Solo, like any daily. Or in co-op, where each teammate owns a different hidden word in the same phrase and neither of you can finish alone.

What it is

The category is crowded with word puzzles, so it isn't competing on puzzles. The job is giving a partner, a sibling, or a long-distance friend a lightweight reason to talk every day. Small persistent groups of two or three, not a social network.

A pnpm TypeScript monorepo: Node and Prisma on Postgres, a React PWA, and native SwiftUI and Kotlin clients, deployed on Fly. More than 1,100 tests across web and native.

The decision

One global daily phrase was serving two incompatible jobs.

The single daily phrase was the identity of the solo game — exactly one puzzle a day, the same one for everyone. It was also where team puzzles came from. That worked right up until a player joined a second team. The moment they see the phrase once, the information asymmetry the entire co-op mechanic depends on is gone: they already know the answer their teammate is working toward. Decoupling team puzzles from the solo daily was the fix.

The more useful part is what pressure-testing changed. I declared my own North Star uncomputable. Anonymous per-device identity splits a returning duo into two separate players, which biases the metric downward — and it does so most severely for exactly the behavior the metric is supposed to reward. A number that is most wrong when you are most successful is worse than no number, So I replaced it at launch with per-game proxies for whether the pair actually felt connected — measurable inside a single game, where identity is reliable.

Two smaller ones. I derived the teams-per-user cap from a comprehension test instead of asserting it, because I wasn't going to ship a confusing product on untested arithmetic. And when I caught myself calling a change "only two lines of code," I split the claim — the genuinely risky part, cross-surface dedupe, had been getting laundered by the cheap headline.

What it cost

Bundling web assets into the native shells means giving up instant web-to-native updates. I took that trade knowingly to avoid a rejection under App Store guideline 4.2, which bars apps that are thin wrappers around a website, and logged live-update infrastructure as deliberate debt rather than pretending it wasn't debt.

Losing the single global phrase also cost the solo game some of its identity. That was the price of the co-op mechanic working at all.

Status & next

iOS build 112 is in TestFlight external review; the Android bundle is on Play's internal track. Public launch is gated on store assets and listing copy, not on engineering.

Account deletion hard-deletes personal data but pseudonymizes shared co-op records to "Former player," so one person leaving can't corrupt their teammate's history.
