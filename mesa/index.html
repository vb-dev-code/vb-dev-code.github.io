Prototype · Expo + Supabase

Mesa Prototype

Declare a goal, check in daily to a private group, and let the group witness follow-through. No progress bars, no scores, no comparison to anyone else.

What it is

Most people who want to change how they eat churn out of tracking apps. Mesa is for groups of close friends rather than a social network, and it covers mixed populations — including people for whom calorie and weight fixation is actively harmful.

Expo and React Native on Supabase with row-level security. All four tabs, six-step onboarding, seven goal types, invite deep-links, and consent gating are built.

The decision

I removed the progress bar on purpose.

Connecting goals to check-ins had five plausible designs. The two numeric ones — goal-type-specific logging, and progress-toward-target bars — are what the category expects, and I killed them on evidence rather than on cost. Showing someone a numeric target at the moment they self-report triggers the violation effect from restraint theory: miss the number and the response is abandonment, not correction. And weight goals plus numeric benchmarking plus social visibility is a documented predictor of disordered eating. Stacking all three would have been the default design.

What shipped is declaration plus a qualitative status. A check-in says "On track" or "Close," carries whatever note the person wants to write, and shows any values they chose to report — but there is no progress bar, no percentage of a target, and no comparison against anyone else. The number, when it appears at all, is something you said, not a score you are being measured against. Weight goals are architecturally isolated from the feed — not hidden behind a setting, structurally unable to reach it — and each goal type has its own rule about whether a target may be displayed at all.

The framing that made the call obvious: Mesa is betting on social witnessing, not social comparison. Those look similar in a mockup and behave nothing alike.

What it cost

Numeric progress bars drive engagement. Removing them moves Mesa off MyFitnessPal's high-engagement, high-churn curve and toward the Focusmate and Strava retention tier. That is a bet, not a fact, and it costs measurable short-term engagement to make it.

The honest problem underneath: the accountability research showing one and a half to three times retention is exercise evidence, not nutrition evidence. Whether it transfers is the keystone assumption the entire model rests on, and it is unproven. I would rather write that down than discover it in month six.

Status & next

Runs end to end in Expo. Not wired: Apple Sign-In, push notifications, server-side EXIF stripping, and the multi-group feed. No store submission.

The next real step is testing the keystone assumption with one real group, not building more surface.
